# Frequently Asked Questions

---

## Coaching and the AI

### Won't the AI just make things up?

This is the right question to ask, and it shaped most of the design. The short answer is: the AI language model in this system is not asked to reason about chess. It is asked to narrate facts that were already computed for it.

Before the language model is called, the system runs Stockfish at depth 12 on the position before and after the player's move. It then uses a separate chess library to extract a precise set of facts from the board: what piece moved and where, whether the destination square was already under attack, which of the player's pieces are left undefended after the move, whether the move gave check, and the same breakdown for the engine's recommended alternative. A complete list of legal moves is also passed to prevent the model from suggesting moves that were not actually available.

The language model receives all of this as structured input and is explicitly instructed not to invent tactical details beyond what it is given. Its job is synthesis and narration, not chess analysis. The chess analysis was already done before the model was ever called.

That said, no system of this kind is perfectly reliable. The model may occasionally phrase something misleadingly or draw an implication the data does not strictly support. The grounding step reduces this risk substantially, but it does not eliminate it entirely.

---

### Why use an AI language model at all? Couldn't you just generate rule-based explanations?

Rule-based systems were the first approach we considered. The problem is that chess positions are combinatorially complex, and a rule-based system produces rigid, repetitive feedback. "You left your bishop undefended" is accurate, but whether that matters depends entirely on the rest of the position. A language model can synthesize multiple inputs at once and produce an explanation that reflects the actual context: the game phase, what the opponent is threatening, what the engine's best alternative was trying to accomplish, and how all of these relate to the specific mistake.

The language model also handles natural variation in how ideas are expressed, which matters for a system used repeatedly. Identical feedback after every blunder would quickly become noise.

---

### How do you know the explanations are actually correct?

The move classification, the best alternative move, the centipawn loss, and all tactical facts (captures, checks, en prise pieces, whether the destination was attacked) are computed deterministically by Stockfish and a chess logic library. These components are not generated by the language model and are not subject to hallucination. They are verified facts drawn directly from the position.

The language model is responsible only for expressing those facts in prose. If the explanation says "your queen was left undefended," it is because the chess library computed that the queen was undefended after the move. The language model did not infer this on its own.

---

### Why are explanations limited to three to five sentences? Is that enough?

In practice, shorter explanations are read more often and acted on more reliably than long ones. A player who just made a move wants to understand what happened before the opponent responds or the next position demands attention. A paragraph-length analysis competes with the board for attention and often gets skipped.

The follow-up question field exists precisely for cases where more depth is wanted. If the initial explanation mentions a concept the player does not recognize, or references a move they want to understand better, they can ask. The follow-up has access to all the same position data as the original explanation and the conversation can continue through multiple exchanges. The system is designed for depth on demand rather than depth by default.

---

### What is Novice Mode actually doing differently?

Novice Mode appends a single additional instruction to the prompt that is sent to the language model, asking it to use beginner-friendly language and define any chess terms it introduces. The core analysis, the move classification, and the tactical facts passed to the model are identical. Only the register of the output changes.

This is intentional. The analysis itself should not be simplified for beginners. A blunder is a blunder regardless of skill level. What changes is whether the explanation assumes the reader already knows what "en prise" or "principal variation" means.

---

### Why does the tutor only analyze my moves and not the bot's?

The coaching purpose of the system is to help you understand your own decisions. Analyzing bot moves in the same way would produce explanations that are interesting but not actionable: the bot made a move, here is why it was strong or weak, but you cannot undo it and it does not reflect a decision you made.

There is also a practical reason. Tutor analysis involves two Stockfish evaluations and a language model call. Running this after every move, including the bot's, would roughly double the analysis load and slow the feedback loop for your own moves.

## The Bot Opponent

### Is the bot actually human-like, or is that just a claim?

The human bot is built on Maia, a neural network trained on tens of millions of games played by real humans on Lichess at a wide range of skill levels. Maia was developed specifically because standard chess engines, even when strength-limited, do not make the kinds of mistakes humans make. A depth-limited Stockfish still finds tactically devastating moves in critical positions; it just finds them slightly slower. Elo-based limiting introduces realistic inaccuracies throughout the game rather than concentrated bursts of brilliance.

The system adds a second layer of human-like behavior through a learned policy that selects among three engines on each move: a weaker version of Maia, a same-strength version of Maia, and an Elo-limited Stockfish. The selection is based on the current position's complexity and the player's chosen skill level. At lower skill settings, the weaker engine is chosen more frequently, producing the kind of inconsistency and occasional blunders that characterize real human play.

---

### What is the point of the Retrieval Bot if you already have the Human Bot?

The two bots represent different approaches to the same goal, and each has distinct properties worth studying.

The Human Bot generates moves dynamically using neural networks and a trained selection policy. Its behavior emerges from learned patterns and is probabilistic: the same position can produce different moves on different runs.

The Retrieval Bot selects moves by searching a database of real human games for positions similar to the current one and voting across what those players actually played. Its moves are grounded in observed human behavior rather than learned representations. At positions the database covers well, it reflects genuine human tendencies with high fidelity. At novel or unusual positions, the retrieval falls back to approximate similarity search, which may be less reliable.

The distinction matters for research questions about human move prediction: one approach models human play statistically, the other retrieves it directly.

---

### Does the bot think in real time, or does it always respond instantly?

The bot applies a delay before returning each move, scaled to its remaining clock time. The formula uses three percent of the bot's remaining seconds, clamped between fifty milliseconds and two seconds. A bot with a full minute remaining will take roughly 1.8 seconds per move. A bot in time pressure will respond in under a tenth of a second. This produces pacing that feels natural without creating unpredictable lag.

The delay is artificial: the move is already computed before the pause begins. The pacing is a deliberate design choice to make the bot feel like a real opponent rather than an instant-response system.

---

### What games are in the Retrieval Bot's database? How current is it?

The database is built from a CSV of real games sourced from Lichess. Each game is expanded into per-position records, and the database is indexed at startup. The currency of the data depends on the specific dataset used; the system does not pull live games.

The retrieval model filters candidates by skill level proximity, weighting games played by users at a similar rating to the target Elo more heavily. It also favors games the player won and games that were rated, on the reasoning that rated games between matched players reflect more deliberate play.

## System Design

### Why run five separate processes instead of one application?

Node.js is highly efficient at managing concurrent connections and API orchestration but is not well suited for the machine learning and engine workloads the system requires. Python has strong support for chess engine bindings, neural network inference, and the libraries used by Maia and the retrieval model. Keeping them separate means each layer operates in its optimal environment.

It also means each service can be restarted, replaced, or scaled independently. If the tutor backend needs to be updated without restarting a game in progress, it can be. If the engine backend crashes, the game and bot continue functioning.

---

### What happens if one of the services goes down during a game?

The system degrades gracefully. If the engine backend is unreachable, the eval bar and best-move arrow stop updating, but the game continues and the bot still plays. If the tutor backend is unreachable, explanations stop appearing, but nothing else is affected. If the playerbot backend is unreachable, the bot stops responding; the player can continue playing against themselves or wait for the service to recover. No part of the system crashes the frontend or corrupts game state.

---

### Why not just use Lichess or Chess.com? They already have analysis tools.

Lichess and Chess.com provide engine analysis after a game is complete, showing you which moves were mistakes in retrospect. This system provides feedback in real time, immediately after each move, while the position is still on the board and the thinking that led to the decision is still fresh. There is a meaningful difference between reviewing a game the next day and understanding a mistake in the moment it happens.

Neither platform offers natural language coaching that responds to follow-up questions in context. The combination of verified tactical grounding, plain-prose explanation, and interactive follow-up is the specific contribution of this system.

---

### Can multiple people use the system at the same time?

Not currently. The Node.js backend holds one game in memory per server instance. A second user connecting to the same server would share that game state. This is listed as a known limitation, and multi-game support backed by persistent storage is identified as a priority for future development.

---

### Is my game data saved anywhere?

No. All game state is held in memory on the Node.js server. Restarting the server clears the current game. Toggle states (Engine on or off, Bot on or off, Tutor on or off, Novice Mode, Elo setting) are saved to browser localStorage and restored on page refresh, but the game itself is not persisted.

## Results and Effectiveness

### What evidence is there that this actually helps players improve?

The system is designed around principles with established support in learning research: immediate feedback is more effective than delayed feedback, explanations grounded in specific events are more actionable than general advice, and interactive follow-up supports deeper understanding than passive reading.

The architecture directly addresses the two failure modes of existing tools: engines that give correct answers without explanation, and static platforms that explain principles without connecting them to the player's own decisions. Whether this translates to measurable rating improvement over time is an open empirical question and an area for future evaluation.

---

### What ELO should I set to get the most out of the system?

Set the bot slightly above your current level, enough to be consistently challenging but not so far above that most games are decided quickly by force. A player who is outmatched in five moves does not get many opportunities for meaningful coaching moments. A player who wins easily does not either.

The rough reference values from the interface:

| Level | ELO Range |
|---|---|
| Beginner | 100 – 800 |
| Amateur | 800 – 1400 |
| Intermediate | 1400 – 1800 |
| Advanced | 1800 – 2200 |
| Expert / Master | 2200+ |

If you are unsure, start at the lower end of the range you think applies to you. Losing instructively is more valuable than winning without challenge.

---

### How should I use the tutor to get the most out of it?

Read every explanation, not just the ones for blunders. Moves classified as fine still have explanations, and understanding why a move was fine reinforces good thinking as much as understanding why a bad move was wrong.

Use the follow-up question field. The initial explanation is short by design. If it references something unfamiliar, ask. If it mentions the engine preferred a different move, ask why. The follow-up has access to everything the original explanation did and the conversation can go several exchanges deep before the context resets.

Enable Novice Mode if any term in an explanation is unfamiliar. It costs nothing and the analysis does not change.

After a loss, use the engine eval bar to find the move where the position shifted decisively, then focus your review there rather than on every mistake equally.
